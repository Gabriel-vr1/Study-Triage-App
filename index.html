AI Transparency Report

How AI was used, what it suggested, and what decisions were made independently by the student.

📋 Overview

Study Triage was built as part of the BET Software Classroom CRASH 2026 competition. The challenge required using an AI tool as an assistant to design and build a simple application that helps a first-year student manage their studies more effectively. AI was used throughout the planning and design process as a thinking partner — not as a code generator. Every formula, every weighting decision, and every feature included in this application was interrogated, modified, and owned by the student before implementation.

🔍 Step 1 — Problem Identification

The student began by identifying real problems first-year university students face rather than jumping directly to a solution. AI was used to brainstorm a broad list of pain points across time management, academic performance, mental load, and study habits. The student then selected the problems that felt most genuine and impactful: not knowing what to study next, studying easy content instead of weak areas, having no big picture view of the semester, and not knowing whether they are actually ready for a test. The AI suggested social and environmental problems such as group project coordination, but the student independently rejected these because the single HTML file format with no network access made them impossible to implement meaningfully.

💡 Step 2 — Concept Development

AI proposed four application categories: a smart priority engine, an exam week scheduler, a sorting algorithm visualiser, and a subject health dashboard. The student chose to combine the priority engine logic with the dashboard format — using the dashboard as the visual shell and the urgency score engine as the brain. The AI also suggested a simpler "ranked list" approach focused only on outputting a sorted task list. The student rejected this because it would not score well on GUI look and feel and would not be visually distinctive from other submissions. The student independently decided that the application needed three connected views — setup, dashboard, and deep dive — so that the sorting algorithm would be meaningful at multiple levels of detail.

⚙️ Step 3 — The Urgency Score Formula

The urgency score formula is the core intellectual contribution of this application. AI was used to identify relevant factors that should influence academic risk: time until test, subject importance, student confidence, and current grade. The student made all weighting decisions independently. Days until test was assigned 40% weight because proximity to a test is the most time-sensitive factor and has an exponential real-world impact. Topic confidence was assigned 25% because it most directly reflects whether a student is prepared for the content. Credit value was assigned 20% because higher credit subjects carry more academic consequence. Grade risk was assigned 15% as a supporting signal rather than a dominant one, since current grade is not always available and can reflect past performance rather than future readiness.

🔢 Step 4 — Sorting Algorithm Choice

The student selected insertion sort after evaluating it against alternatives. AI confirmed that insertion sort is appropriate for small datasets, is stable, and produces a readable comparator function that judges can follow. The student independently decided that the sort should run visibly on a recalculate action rather than silently in the background, because the rubric specifically scores sorting algorithm implementation and the sort needed to be demonstrable. The sort was hand-written without using JavaScript's built-in array.sort() method, ensuring full transparency of the algorithm logic.

🎨 Step 5 — Design and UI Decisions

AI suggested keeping the visual design minimal to reduce complexity. The student disagreed and chose a dark dashboard aesthetic with colour-coded risk indicators, a score breakdown view, and a semester timeline. This decision was made because GUI look and feel accounts for 10 out of 30 available marks, and a plain ranked list would not score near the maximum. The colour system — red for critical, amber for watch, green for on track — was chosen because it mirrors real-world risk dashboards and is immediately readable without explanation.

🚫 Step 6 — What AI Did Not Do

AI did not independently choose the application concept. AI did not determine the formula weights. AI did not decide which features to include or exclude. AI did not write the final code without student direction at each stage. Every stage of development was initiated by the student with a specific goal, and AI output was reviewed, questioned, and modified before being accepted.

📐 Formula Reference

The urgency score formula for reference:

Urgency Score =
  (Days Until Test Score  × 0.40) +
  (Credit Value Score    × 0.20) +
  (Confidence Score     × 0.25) +
  (Grade Risk Score     × 0.15)

Result: 0–100  |  75–100 = Critical  |  45–74 = Watch  |  0–44 = On Track

✅ Student Declaration

I used AI as a design and thinking partner throughout this project. I asked questions, challenged its suggestions, rejected ideas that did not serve the brief, and made all final decisions independently. The urgency formula weights, the feature set, the sorting algorithm choice, and the visual design direction were all my own decisions. The code was written with AI assistance at each stage, but every line was reviewed and understood before inclusion. This application represents my own thinking about a real problem, built with AI as a tool — not as a replacement for judgment.
